⚠ Safety. This is a backup aid, not a lifeline. Phone GPS can fail under dense canopy and batteries die. Still carry a power bank, tell someone your route and return time, and bring a real compass. Don't rely on the phone alone deep in the jungle.
🔋 Stretch your battery further

Biggest savers, in order: switch this app to Saver mode above; turn on the phone's Airplane mode once your offline map tiles are downloaded (GPS still works without signal, but the phone stops burning power hunting for a tower); drop screen brightness and let it auto-lock — tracking keeps running with the screen off; close other apps; keep the phone warm (cold kills battery fast). A cheap 10,000 mAh power bank roughly triples your safe time in the field.

🚀 Quick start

The whole workflow, in order. Do the one-time setup at home on wifi.

① Right away (no setup): satellite photo

Open the Map tab and tap Satellite photo — real imagery of the ground, no account needed. Use it to see bare/rocky areas, rivers, tracks, old workings and how to get in. The Reddish-ground hint roughly highlights rusty patches (possible iron — but ground-check).

② Optional upgrade: real analysis layers — full setup walkthrough

This unlocks genuine iron/clay/radar band-math. It's free but the Copernicus website is confusing, so follow these exactly. Do it at home on wifi — allow about 20 minutes. You only do this once.

PART A — Create the account
This is where most people get stuck. Copernicus redesign the site often and there is no obvious "sign up" button. Do not go straight to a dashboard, account or login URL — with no account yet it just bounces you to a login screen with no way to sign up.

1. Go to dataspace.copernicus.eu
2. Try the blue Get started button on the homepage — on the current design this is the most likely route to signup.
3. If that doesn't lead anywhere useful, click LOGIN (top right) → Your profile. That opens the login screen, which normally has a Register / Create account link on the form itself.
  Note: the LOGIN dropdown itself has no Register option — it only lists places to log in to (Your profile, openEO, Sentinel Hub). Don't hunt for Register in there.
4. Fill in the required fields, tick accept terms and conditions and the privacy policy, then submit.
5. Open the verification email and click "Verify email address". Your account is not active until you do this — the most commonly missed step.

Stuck? Copernicus support: help-login@dataspace.copernicus.eu

Shortcut — skip all of Part A: if someone has already set up an instance ID, just use theirs. One ID works on more than one phone and avoids this entire process. Only register your own if you want your own quota.

Once you have an account, the quickest way back to the dashboard is LOGIN → Sentinel Hub from the homepage.

PART B — Get your instance ID
1. Go to shapps.dataspace.copernicus.eu/dashboard/#/configurations
2. Log in with the account from Part A
3. You'll see a list of configurations. Either use the ready-made "Simple WMS Instance", or click New configuration, name it (e.g. ProspectField), and base it on the Simple WMS template
4. Click into that configuration. The instance ID is the long code shown (like c32c21b1-84ac-…). It may appear inside a URL like .../wms/THE-CODE — you want just the code after /wms/, not the whole address
5. Copy it → in this app go to Map → open 🔬 Advanced analysis layers → paste into the box
6. Tap 🔎 Test my ID. If it shows an image, you're connected.

PART C — Add the layers
Inside your configuration, click ＋ New layer for each one below. Name them exactly as shown (capitals and hyphens matter — the app looks for these names), pick the data source, then Save.

• SENTINEL-1 — data source: Sentinel-1 GRD. If it warns "please select predefined product or enter your processing script", just pick any predefined product (e.g. VV or VV+VH decibel gamma0). This is your radar layer.
• IRON-RATIO — data source: Sentinel-2 L2A, Data Processing → Custom script → paste the iron script
• FERROUS-IRON — data source: Sentinel-2 L2A → Custom script → paste the ferrous script
• ALTERATION — data source: Sentinel-2 L2A → Custom script → paste the alteration script

The three custom scripts are in the next section below — copy each one into its layer.

If something doesn't work
• Nothing happens when you tap a layer — you're zoomed too far out. Satellite data won't serve at whole-country scale; zoom into a town-sized area.
• Optical layers blank but radar works — that's cloud, which is normal in Malaysia. Radar sees through it.
• Error 400 — usually a layer name that doesn't match exactly. Check capitals and hyphens against the list above.
• Test my ID fails — check the ID is just the code, with no spaces or extra URL text.

②b The three custom scripts (copy these into Copernicus)

Paste each into its matching layer's Custom script box in the Configuration Utility.

IRON-RATIO — bright red = rusty, iron-stained ground & gossans

FERROUS-IRON — a second, different iron signature

ALTERATION — red=iron, green=clay, blue=ferrous. Reddish-yellow = iron+clay together, the classic alteration signature

Tap a Copy button, then paste into the matching layer's Custom script box in Copernicus. (You can also tap a script box to select it manually.)

③ At home on wifi: scout from space

On the Map tab, pan to your partner's licence block. With the advanced layers on: Iron and Clay show possible targets, Radar shows faults through cloud, Veg gaps finds bare ground you can inspect. Note the promising spots. (No Copernicus ID yet? The plain satellite photo still helps you plan.)

④ Still on wifi: download the map

Same Map tab → pan/zoom to your target area → Download current view for offline. Now the map works with no signal in the field. Do this for every area you plan to walk.

⑤ Before you walk in: tell someone

Trail tab → set your contact's WhatsApp number, name, and return time → tap “Heading in” while you still have signal (usually at the vehicle). This is the most important step. Then tap Start tracking and pick Balanced battery mode.

⑥ On site: estimate, sample, photograph

At a promising spot, use the Estimate tab to score it. Worth sampling? Sample tab → capture GPS, add photos, tag what you see. High-scoring spots are the ones to send for lab assay later.

⑦ Getting out: retrace & check in

Trail tab → Retrace to start follows your exact breadcrumb path back. When you reach the vehicle and have signal, tap “I'm out safe” to stand your contact down.

⑧ After: confirm with a lab

Satellite hints and field scores are not proof. Send your best samples to an accredited lab (JMG or a commercial assay lab). Positive results → bring in a licensed exploration geologist and follow the official protocol with your partner.

⚠ Cloud warning. Malaysia's constant cloud often blocks the optical layers (iron, clay, veg). Radar always works. If an optical layer looks blank, that's cloud — try radar or check back another day.

🎓 Start here — how prospecting actually works

If you're new to this, read this once. It explains why we look for the things we look for.

The one idea that explains everything

Valuable metals — gold, silver, copper, lead, zinc — usually form deep underground as sulphide minerals (metals combined with sulphur). Hot fluids carry them up through cracks in the rock and deposit them there.

You almost never see the metal itself. What you look for instead are the side-effects those hot fluids left behind: quartz veins, altered rock, and rusty staining. Prospecting is detective work — you're reading clues, not spotting treasure.

Why rusty ground (iron oxide) matters so much

Sulphides are unstable at the surface. Over thousands of years rain and oxygen break them down, and the iron inside them turns to rust-coloured iron oxide that stays behind as a weathered cap. That cap is called a gossan.

So the reasoning is: rusty staining → sulphides were once here → sulphides often carry gold or other metals → worth investigating.

It's one of the oldest prospecting clues there is, used for centuries before any technology. It's also visible from space, which is exactly why this app has an iron-oxide satellite layer.

⚠ But most rusty rock is just rusty rock

This is the mistake almost every beginner makes. Iron is one of the most common elements on Earth, so red-brown staining is everywhere for completely boring reasons — ordinary iron minerals weathering, tropical laterite soil (very common in Malaysia), or plain rust.

A real gossan has features ordinary staining doesn't:
• Boxwork / spongy texture — a honeycomb of small cavities left behind where sulphide crystals dissolved away. This is the giveaway. Ordinary rusty soil is solid; a gossan looks cellular, almost like a sponge or a wasp nest.
• Sits with quartz veining — on or beside a vein, not spread evenly across a hillside.
• Odd colours mixed in — greens or blues (copper), sooty black (manganese), yellows. Multiple colours beat uniform red.
• Heavy for its size — often denser than it looks.

Bright colour alone means nothing. Uniform red-brown covering a whole slope is almost always just laterite soil.

The real rule: look for agreement, not one clue

No single indicator means much on its own. What counts is several pointing the same way in the same place:

iron staining + quartz veining + a fault or rock contact + sulphide traces

That combination is worth a proper sample and a lab test. One rusty rock is not.

This is exactly why the Estimate tab needs several ticked indicators before it scores high — it's designed to stop you chasing single clues.

What "confirms" a find (and what doesn't)

Nothing you can see confirms anything. Not the satellite layers, not the field indicators, not a high score in this app. All of those do one job: narrow thousands of hectares down to a few spots worth sampling.

Only a laboratory assay (ICP or fire assay) tells you whether metal is actually present and how much. And even a good assay is just one point — proving a deposit takes systematic sampling and eventually drilling, with a licensed geologist involved.

Think of it this way: this app helps you decide where to spend your sampling money. It cannot tell you what's in the rock.

Words you'll keep seeing

Ore — rock with enough valuable metal to be worth mining.
Sulphide — metal combined with sulphur; how most metals occur underground.
Gossan — rusty weathered cap over oxidised sulphides.
Alteration — rock chemically changed by hot fluids; often clay-rich and paler.
Vein — a mineral-filled crack, commonly quartz.
Outcrop — bedrock exposed at surface, still in place.
Float — loose rock that has moved from its source; a clue, but not proof of what's underneath.
Contact — where two rock types meet; a favourable place for ore.
Assay — lab test measuring how much metal is in a sample.
Placer — metal concentrated in stream gravels by water.
Pathfinder — an easy-to-spot clue that points to something harder to see.

📖 Malaysian field indicators

Surface clues and the minerals they point to in Malaysian geology.

Gossan / iron capping

→ gold, base metals

Rusty red-brown weathered rock (oxidised sulphides). Classic pathfinder over buried mineralisation. Sample it and nearby quartz veins.
Tell it from ordinary rust: a real gossan has a spongy boxwork texture — small cavities left where sulphide crystals dissolved — and usually sits with quartz veining. Uniform red-brown over a whole slope is normally just laterite soil.

Quartz veins at granite/schist contact

→ lode gold, tungsten

The Central Gold Belt (Pahang–Kelantan–Terengganu) hosts gold in quartz veins near granite margins. Look for milky-grey vuggy quartz.

Black heavy sand in stream traps

→ tin (cassiterite), gold, monazite (REE)

Pan the concentrate. Alluvial cassiterite is widespread; monazite in the black-sand fraction carries rare earths.

Weathered granite / laterite

→ ion-adsorption REE

Deeply weathered granite can concentrate rare earths in clay. No reliable visual signature — sample clay-rich saprolite and assay.

Limestone / marble contacts (skarn)

→ iron, copper, gems

Intrusion–limestone contacts (Kinta Valley, Langkawi) can host skarn minerals and corundum.

Galena & sulphide veins

→ silver (with lead & zinc)

Heavy silvery-grey galena that breaks into cubes is the main silver carrier — silver is rarely visible metal. Look for quartz veins speckled with metallic sulphides, banded/crustiform textures, and black manganese staining. In Malaysia silver is usually a byproduct of polymetallic systems, not a target on its own.

⚠ Reality check. None of these confirm an economic deposit. They tell you where to take a proper sample for lab geochemistry (ICP / fire assay). Field work narrows targets — assays and drilling prove them.

🛰️ Reading the satellite layers

What each Map-tab layer shows and how to read it. All are hints that narrow where to walk — never proof. Cloud blocks the optical ones; radar always works.

🌍 Satellite photo / True colour

→ the lie of the land

Real imagery. Spot bare rock, rivers, tracks, old workings and access routes. Your planning base layer.

🟠 Iron ratio

→ gossans, iron staining (gold pathfinder)

Bright red-orange = rusty, iron-stained ground. Gossans cap many gold and base-metal deposits, so bright patches are priority spots to sample.

🔶 Ferrous iron

→ a second iron signature

Picks up different iron minerals than the iron ratio. When both light up in the same place, the signal is stronger.

🟫 Clay / SWIR & 🌈 Alteration composite

→ hydrothermal alteration

Clays and altered rock surround many ore bodies. On the composite, reddish-yellow = iron + clay together, the classic alteration signature. Investigate where iron and clay overlap.

📡 Radar

→ faults & structure (vein control)

Sees through cloud. Straight bright/dark lines = ridges and faults. Gold veins often follow faults, so trace these lineaments — especially where they cross an iron/alteration patch.

🌿 Veg gaps

→ where rock is exposed

Orange/tan = bare or rocky ground you can actually inspect on foot. Green = jungle hiding the geology.

The strongest target is where several layers agree: an iron + clay patch, on or near a radar fault line, on bare ground. That's the spot to sample first. At 20 m resolution these find districts, not single veins — and only a lab assay confirms anything.

⚖️ Before you dig — protocol

Licensing & land

Confirm your partner's licence covers the exact block you're on. Mineral rights sit with state authorities (State Director of Lands & Mines / JMG). Sampling outside the licensed area can be an offence.

Keeping it quiet, safely

Solo recon is fine for finding targets, but tell one trusted person your daily route and expected return. Remote sites, rivers and old workings are genuinely dangerous — discretion should never mean nobody knows where you are.

When you find something

Don't over-collect or start earthworks. Record location + photos, take a representative sample, send to an accredited lab (JMG or commercial). Positive results → bring in a licensed exploration geologist and follow official reporting with your partner.

Old mine hazards

Abandoned tin/gold workings have collapse, deep water, and sometimes mercury/arsenic. Don't enter shafts or adits alone.
